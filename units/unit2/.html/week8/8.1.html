  • Describe and differentiate the components of a problem specification: 

    • Problem statement

  • Analyse how a well-written specification supports the success of a software solution. 

Every successful computational solution begins with a clear understanding of the problem. Just like a blueprint guides the construction of a building, a problem specification guides the development of a software solution. It helps avoid ambiguity, ensures the client’s needs are fully understood, and provides a benchmark against which the final product can be evaluated. 

In real-world software development, failing to define the problem accurately often leads to wasted time, budget overruns, and unsatisfactory results. A well-written specification ensures that all stakeholders—developers, users, clients, and testers—have a shared understanding of what the system is meant to do and under what conditions it must operate. 

Let’s consider a brief case study to highlight this: 

Case Study: The Cafeteria Queue Crisis

A large school with over 1,000 students faced long lunchtime queues at its cafeteria. The administration wanted a digital solution to help students pre-order meals and reduce congestion. 

A group of students offered to build an app—but they jumped straight into coding. After two weeks, they had a working app, but it only supported pre-ordering during lunch hours (not before), didn’t allow custom meal preferences, and had no way to notify users when their food was ready. The system failed during testing and was eventually scrapped. 

What went wrong? They skipped the problem specification. They didn’t define the actual user needs, constraints (e.g., device compatibility), input/output requirements, or success criteria. A clear specification at the start would have identified that the app must support morning pre-orders, track dietary preferences, and provide queue notifications.

 

 This example shows how essential it is to step back and ask the right questions before building a solution. In the context of computational thinking, this step is known as problem specification, and it forms the foundation for everything that follows—decomposition, abstraction, algorithmic thinking, and evaluation. 

In this lesson, you will learn how to create a full problem specification using a structured approach that includes problem statements, constraints, goals, inputs and outputs, and evaluation criteria. These skills will not only guide your approach in projects and internal assessments but also prepare you for solving complex real-world problems effectively. 

 

What is a Problem Specification?

A problem specification is a structured, detailed, and unambiguous description of the problem that needs to be solved. It serves as a foundational document that guides the entire computational thinking process—from understanding the user’s needs to designing, implementing, testing, and evaluating a working solution. 

Unlike a casual explanation or a vague brief, a problem specification breaks down the problem in formal terms, outlines the expectations, and sets boundaries for what the final system should and should not do. In doing so, it helps identify: 

  • What exactly the system must solve 
  • The limits and constraints within which it must operate 
  • The features and functionalities it must include 
  • How the success of the solution will be determined 

Why is it necessary? 

Without a specification, developers often face miscommunication, scope creep, or failure to meet user needs. A strong specification acts like a contract between the developer and the user, ensuring everyone has the same expectations. 

Scenario: A Misunderstood Chatbot 

Imagine a student volunteers to create a simple chatbot to help their school office respond to basic queries (like school timings, event dates, or office hours). The student starts coding based on their own assumptions. After completing the bot, the office staff test it and find that: 

  • It doesn’t understand alternative phrasing (e.g., “What time does school start?” vs. “When should I arrive at school?”) 
  • It gives incorrect responses on holidays 
  • It cannot escalate queries it doesn't understand 

Had the student created a problem specification first, they would have known to include: 

  • Support for multiple user input styles (natural language processing) 
  • Integration with a holiday calendar 
  • A fallback or escalation feature for unsupported queries 

 

Specification as a Bridge to Design and Evaluation 

A well-crafted specification leads directly into: 

  • System design, by clarifying what features need to be included 
  • Testing, by defining success criteria and edge cases 
  • Evaluation, by providing benchmarks against which the final product can be judged 

 

In the IB Computer Science course, especially when you undertake the Internal Assessment (IA) project, constructing a clear and complete problem specification is the first and most critical step. It ensures that your planning, development, and evaluation are all aligned with the real needs of your client or end user. 

  • Who will use the system?  
  • What information needs to be tracked? 
  • What hardware or software limitations exist? 
  • How will success be measured? 

 

Problem Specification

Components of a Problem Specification 

A comprehensive problem specification includes several key components, each serving a distinct purpose. Together, they provide the foundation for effective system design and implementation. In this section, we will break down the five major components using two consistent real-world examples to help you relate theory to practice.

 

a)  Problem Statement

The problem statement is the starting point of any specification. It defines the problem in precise terms, outlines who is experiencing the problem, and establishes the context in which it occurs. A strong problem statement avoids assumptions or proposed solutions—it focuses only on clearly articulating what the problem is. 

A good problem statement should answer: 

  • What is the issue? 
  • Who is affected by it? 
  • Why does it matter? 

Example 1: Local Bakery 

The bakery currently takes orders over the phone or in person, leading to incomplete records, confusion about daily production needs, and occasional missed or duplicate orders. There is no centralized system for tracking what has been ordered, by whom, or for when. As a result, customers experience delays and the bakery struggles with overproduction or underproduction each day. 

 Example 2: Healthcare Clinic

The clinic uses a manual appointment logbook, which often results in scheduling conflicts and missed follow-ups for patients. There is no digital tracking of patient visits or automated reminders. This inefficiency affects patient care and creates administrative burdens for the front desk staff, who spend hours managing paper-based records. 

A clear problem statement helps identify the scope and urgency of the issue without jumping to how the system will solve it. 

 

b) Constraints and Limitations 

Constraints define the boundaries within which a solution must operate. These include: 

  • Technical constraints: hardware or software limitations 
  • User constraints: needs or characteristics of users (e.g., digital literacy, language) 
  • Legal and ethical constraints: privacy laws, accessibility regulations 
  • Budgetary/time constraints: deadlines, available resources 

Understanding constraints ensures that the solution is realistic, inclusive, and compliant. 

Example 1: Local Bakery 

The bakery staff are not tech-savvy; the system must be extremely user-friendly with minimal training required. 

The solution must work on a single point-of-sale tablet already used at the counter. 

Due to budget constraints, the bakery prefers open-source tools and cannot afford recurring subscription costs. 

Example 2: Healthcare Clinic 

The clinic must comply with local patient data protection regulations (e.g., HIPAA or its local equivalent). 

Internet connectivity in the clinic is unreliable, so the system should work offline and sync data when online. 

The clinic operates with limited funding and cannot afford commercial scheduling software; a lightweight web or desktop app is preferable. 

Constraints prevent wasted development effort and help guide appropriate design decisions from the start.

 

c) Objectives and Goals 

Objectives define the functionality the system must offer. These are usually high-level, action-based requirements (e.g., allow users to register). 

Goals, on the other hand, define how well these objectives should be met, typically in terms of performance, efficiency, and usability. 

Together, they form the success targets for your solution.  

Example 1: Local Bakery 

Objectives: 

  • Accept customer pre-orders online, capturing item, quantity, customer name, contact, and pick-up time 
  • Enable staff to view all pending orders for the current and next day 
  • Allow customers to modify or cancel orders before a cutoff time 

Goals: 

  • All orders should be viewable in a dashboard, grouped by pick-up date and time 
  • Customers should receive order confirmation instantly via SMS or email 
  • The system should process and display updates within 2 seconds of any change 

Example 2: Healthcare Clinic 

Objectives: 

Schedule patient appointments digitally and avoid overlaps 

Automatically notify patients about upcoming visits or missed follow-ups 

Track patient visits and allow staff to add quick notes for each visit 

Goals: 

Appointment confirmations must be sent within 30 seconds of booking 

The system should flag patients who haven't visited in over 6 months 

Front desk staff should be able to book or modify appointments in under 60 seconds 

Using SMART criteria (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) to write goals can ensure clarity and testability. 

 

d) Input and Output Specifications 

This component outlines exactly what data the system will receive (input) and what it should produce (output). These definitions guide database design, user interface elements, and processing logic. 

It is vital to be specific about: 

  • The format of input (e.g., text, dropdown, date) 
  • Source of input (user-entered, sensor, file, etc.) 
  • Output format (report, message, chart, printed slip, etc.) 
  • Frequency or timing of outputs (real-time, daily, on request) 

Example 1: Local Bakery 

Input: 

  • Customer name (text), contact (email/phone), item(s) ordered (dropdown or multi-select), quantity (number), preferred pickup time (time picker) 

Output: 

  • Order summary page for customer 
  • Daily production report grouped by item and quantity 
  • Notification message to customer (SMS/email) confirming order and pickup time 

 

Example 2: Healthcare Clinic 

Input: 

  • Patient name (text), ID (number), contact details, preferred doctor (dropdown), appointment date and time (date/time picker), visit reason (text) 

Output: 

  • Daily appointment schedule for each doctor 
  • SMS/email reminders to patients 24 hours before appointments 
  • Alerts for missed appointments or long gaps since last visit 
Accurate input/output design is essential for building the system's user interface and backend logic.  

 

e) Evaluation Criteria 

Evaluation criteria define how the success of the final solution will be measured. These criteria should directly reflect the objectives and goals, and include both functional testing (Does it work?) and non-functional testing (How well does it work?). 

Consider: 

  • Accuracy of outputs 
  • Speed of operation 
  • User satisfaction 
  • Compliance with constraints (e.g., accessibility, legal) 
  • Reliability and robustness under typical use 

Example 1: Local Bakery 

  • The system should correctly display all pre-orders by 6:00 AM each day 
  • At least 95% of customers should report that placing an order was “easy” or “very easy” in post-purchase feedback 
  • No more than 2 missed or duplicate orders should be reported in a given week 

Example 2: Healthcare Clinic 

  • 100% of appointments must be unique—no overlapping times for the same doctor 
  • Appointment reminders should be received at least 24 hours before scheduled time 
  • The system should remain responsive (<3 seconds delay) even with 100+ records 
Well-defined evaluation criteria enable you to plan effective testing and validation. They also help justify your design choices during project evaluation (especially for the IA). 

 

Importance

Why Does Specification Matter? 

Real-world project failures often result from poorly defined specifications. Famous examples include: 

  • The UK NHS IT failure due to unclear requirements 
  • The Mars Climate Orbiter crash due to a mismatch in unit conventions 

Good specifications help: 

  • Save development time 
  • Align stakeholder expectations 
  • Provide clarity for testing and debugging
