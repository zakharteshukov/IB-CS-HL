Computing devices perform a variety of tasks, from simple operations like browsing the internet to complex simulations in artificial intelligence and video game rendering. The Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) plays a crucial role in accelerating computational tasks that require parallel processing, working alongside the Central Processing Unit (CPU) to enhance performance. This lesson explores the role of GPUs, their architectural advantages, and the key differences between CPUs and GPUs, helping students understand how modern computers handle demanding workloads.

Lesson

The Role of GPUs

 

What is a GPU?

A Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) is a specialized processor designed to handle parallel computations efficiently. While initially built to render images and videos, modern GPUs are widely used for complex computational tasks such as machine learning, scientific simulations, and cryptocurrency mining. 

 

GPU Architecture & Processing Power

Parallel Processing

Unlike a CPU, which has a few powerful cores optimized for sequential processing, a GPU contains thousands of smaller cores that process multiple tasks simultaneously.

Optimized for Floating-Point Operations

GPUs excel in operations requiring massive mathematical computations, such as physics simulations and deep learning model training.

High-Bandwidth Memory (VRAM)

GPUs use fast memory to process large datasets quickly, which is essential for real-time rendering and AI applications.

  

Real-World Applications of GPUs 

  • Video Game Rendering: GPUs generate realistic graphics by performing real-time shading, texture mapping, and lighting calculations.
  • Artificial Intelligence & Machine Learning: Many AI models, particularly deep learning networks, rely on GPUs for training and inference due to their ability to process massive datasets.
  • Medical Imaging & Simulations: GPUs enable high-speed medical diagnostics, such as MRI scans and CT image processing.
  • Cryptocurrency Mining: GPUs solve cryptographic puzzles efficiently due to their parallel computing power.
  • Weather & Scientific Simulations: Climate models and particle physics experiments require high-speed calculations that GPUs handle efficiently.

Summary of GPU Role and Applications

Architecture  Thousands of small cores designed for parallel processing.
