Introduction

In the previous lesson, you explored what a problem specification is and how each component contributes to the success of a computational solution. You examined how a well-defined specification helps align user needs with technical feasibility and sets the foundation for clear design, testing, and evaluation. 

In this lesson, you will apply that knowledge by constructing a full problem specification for a given scenario. This exercise simulates a real-world development situation where you must think critically and systematically. It is also designed to build your readiness for the Internal Assessment (IA), where writing a strong, comprehensive, and testable specification is essential. By engaging in this activity, you will not only deepen your conceptual understanding but also practice an important skill that underpins much of the project-based work in the IB Computer Science course. 

Scenario

Scenario: Community Recycling Hub Scheduler 

You are approached by a local environmental non-profit that manages a community recycling hub serving several neighbourhoods. The hub sees a steady increase in residents bringing recyclables—ranging from plastics and electronics to large household items. With no digital system in place, appointments are booked using handwritten logs or informal phone calls. 

This manual process often results in residents arriving at the same time, causing confusion, extended wait times, and unnecessary congestion at the site. Staff find it difficult to maintain an accurate schedule and are spending excessive time resolving booking conflicts and managing inquiries. 

The organisation is seeking a lightweight digital solution that will allow residents to self-schedule recycling drop-offs in available time slots. It should also enable staff to easily view, manage, and update bookings as needed, all while being simple enough to use without technical training.

Your Task 

Using the scenario above, construct a complete problem specification. Your work should include the following sections: 

 

1. Problem Statement 

  • Clearly define the core problem. 
  • Identify the stakeholders involved (e.g., residents, staff). 
  • Outline the broader context of the issue. 

2. Constraints and Limitations 

  • Consider technical, financial, user-based, or legal constraints. 
  • Think about devices, internet availability, user ability, and software preferences. 

3. Objectives and Goals 

  • What should the solution achieve? 
  • What measurable performance targets should it meet? 

4. Input and Output Specification 

  • Define exactly what the user will provide (input). 
  • Define what the system will return or produce (output). 
  • Be specific about formats, timing, and data types. 

5. Evaluation Criteria 

  • How will you determine whether the system is successful? 
  • Think about usability, speed, accuracy, and reliability. 

 

 Support and Scaffolding 

  • Use the bakery and healthcare clinic examples from Lesson 8.1 as reference templates. 
  • You may draft each section individually and then compile them. 
  • Share your draft with a peer for comments or use the self-evaluation checklist provided. 

 

Self-Evaluation Checklist: 

  • Does my problem statement clearly describe the issue, the stakeholders, and the context? 
  • Have I identified at least three meaningful constraints or limitations relevant to the scenario? 
  • Are the objectives of the system functional and aligned with the problem? 
  • Are the goals specific, measurable, and realistically achievable? 
  • Have I clearly listed all necessary inputs, with formats and sources? 
  • Are the expected outputs described accurately and completely? 
  • Do my evaluation criteria address functionality, usability, and reliability? 
  • Is my language precise, unambiguous, and free from unnecessary technical jargon? 
  • Have I demonstrated how the components of my specification connect logically? 
  • Would someone unfamiliar with the problem understand the system from reading my specification? 

 

Optional Extension 

As an extension activity, I would like you to think creatively about how your solution could go a step further. Propose two to three features that go beyond the core scheduling function. These could include things like auto-reminder notifications, contactless check-in options, or categorizing recyclables to optimize sorting. 

When doing so, make sure to briefly explain why each proposed feature could be beneficial. Consider how it would help users (residents or staff), improve efficiency, or contribute to environmental goals. You don’t need to implement these features, but showing that you can think beyond the minimum requirements is a valuable skill in system design. 

 

Conclusion 

This task bridges theoretical understanding and real-world application. Writing an effective problem specification now will give you a practical edge in preparing your IA and tackling future software design tasks, particularly where you must meet client needs through structured analysis and careful planning. 

This is more than just an academic exercise—it reflects the same process professional developers follow when planning real software projects. The ability to construct a well-structured specification can help you communicate your ideas clearly, reduce misunderstandings with clients or users, and serve as a solid foundation for your design and testing stages. 

Treat this as both a challenge and a rehearsal for the kind of critical thinking and planning you will need as you progress through this course and beyond.
